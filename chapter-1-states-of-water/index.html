On a bright summer afternoon, Aavi and Thirav enjoy their shikanji (lemonade). Looking at the ice in their glasses, Thirav wonders — are ice and water the same substance or different ones? Aavi disagrees with Thirav. What do you think?

Evaporation — Water's Disappearing Act

One rainy morning, Aavi and Thirav notice water puddles in the playground. By evening, the puddles had partly disappeared! Where did the water go?

Evaporation is the process of conversion of water into its vapour (gas) state. It happens even at room temperature — not just when water boils!

What makes evaporation faster?

☀️ More heatWater in sunlight evaporates faster than in shade
💨 Moving airWind carries vapour away, so more water evaporates
📐 Larger areaSpread on a plate dries faster than in a bottle cap
💧 Low humidityDry air absorbs more vapour than already-humid air
Cooling effect: When water evaporates from your skin (sweat), it takes heat away — that's why you feel cooler under a fan, and why a matka (earthen pot) keeps water cold!
🥤

Condensation — The Mystery of the Wet Glass

Aavi and Thirav add ice to a glass of lemonade. After a few minutes, they see water droplets on the outside of the glass — even though they never poured water there! Where did these drops come from?

Condensation is the process of conversion of water vapour (gas) into its liquid state. Water vapour in the air touches the cold glass and turns back into water droplets. This is the same reason we see dew drops on plants in the morning!

Proof it's condensation, not leaking:

If you mark the water level inside the glass with a tape and observe — the level inside does not drop, but water still appears on the outside. This proves the water comes from the air, not from inside the glass!

The Water Cycle — Animated

Watch how water continuously moves between Earth and the sky.

1. Evaporation The sun heats water in oceans, lakes, and rivers. Water turns into invisible water vapour and rises up.
2. Condensation As water vapour rises, air gets cooler. Vapour condenses around dust particles to form tiny water droplets — these are clouds!
3. Precipitation Droplets join together and get heavy. They fall as rain, hail, or snow back to Earth's surface.
4. Collection Water collects in oceans, rivers, and underground. The whole cycle starts again — forever!
